Morality

The term morality comes from the Latin mores which means morals, it means a set of norms and rules relating to good and evil, specific to a society or a group. In philosophy, morality tries to define what is the purpose of human action to deduce the conduct to be held.

Morality

Morality is the differentiation of intentions, decisions, and actions between those that are distinguished as proper and those that are improper.Additionally, morality would be viewed as a secondary social product and would serve primarily as a social cohesion principle. However, this very real morality determination does not reveal everything about its social function: How can we specifically explain the fact that morality can lead us to act against the direct interests of the social structure that created it? This is due to the fact that morality cannot be reduced to the conditions under which it emerged, even when it is determined; Its goal is always bigger than the social life it builds.

Morality

More fundamentally, the question of whether being able to take into account moral requirements has any significance for social life and in what order this supposed significance is the question of the usefulness of morality for social life. It is not just a question of whether or not it is useful to act appropriately; rather, the question is whether or not it is useful to do so. Does it, for instance, improve citizenship? Is it happier for the social body? Etc. In a nutshell, the purpose of this inquiry is to inquire into the means by which an individual's social existence is able to express what appears to be the core of who they are. And how does this internal-external relationship, which is played on an individual level, affect the social group's life?.
